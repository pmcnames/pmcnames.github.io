A DIGITAL ZINE
BY PEARL MCNAMES
introduction

This is a digital zine about how you can physically document your personal experiences for the sake of future recollection.

If you've ever read childhood journal entries, you know the joy of getting a glimpse into the inner workings of your young mind. Reminiscing can bring up strange, surprising, and sometimes bittersweet feelings. You might realize how disconnected you are from that side of yourself now, or simply enjoy the reminder of how carefree you used to be. Saving these items helps repair the fragile connection between past and present.

In fifteen years, how well will you understand your present self? You may remember the names of friends, experiences that stand out in your mind, the general layout of your current dwelling. But will you know what was important to you? What you did on a day-to-day basis? What you struggled with? It’s easy to lose track of who you used to be, despite its fundamental connection to who you’ve become.

Consider what you currently have to look back on. Your phone’s camera roll, Spotify playlists, Pinterest boards, social media feeds... Does this paint an accurate picture of your life? With many of these being public, how will you understand who you were when you weren’t performing for other people? With the digital dark age upon us, these things could easily be lost to time.

Additionally, digital media fails to maintain all the sensory elements of physical media. Objects contain textures, smells, sometimes even tastes that bring back richer memories.

note

I recommend trying whatever formats appeal to you and combining them as needed. Personally I keep a combined journal / commonplace / scrapbook, a sketchbook, two small notebooks (one next to my bed for late-night ideas, one in my bag for when I’m out on the town), and separate collections of letters, recipes, etc. It’s an imperfect and constantly evolving system.

I started journaling a few years ago after reading part of The Artist’s Way. Julia Cameron suggests doing “Morning Pages” at the beginning every day as part of a routine to promote creativity. The goal is to create a direct mind-to-page pathway without pause for thought. You write absolutely whatever comes to mind in real time, even if that means stopping in the middle of a sentence to begin another thought. Even if it’s just nonsense. I found that I almost always wrote about recent events because that was simply the easiest thing to do. The more you allow yourself to create freely without criticizing, the easier it becomes. Make it messy. Let it be a pile of cringe-worthy garbage. You’ll still be glad you took the time to write it down.  There are a lot of benefits to writing as a means of processing your emotions, but I’ll resist the urge to preach about this. I’ll just say that beyond its historical value, it’s a great way to self-regulate and grow.

There are endless lists of journal prompts online, though I think the best way is to just write whatever comes to the surface. Regardless, here are a few ideas to use as a jumping off point.

what to write
  • Thoughts on movies/tv shows you’ve seen, books you’ve read
  • Plans for the upcoming season, things you want to do
  • Roses and thorns (positive and negative parts of your life)
  • Dreams you’ve had
  • Reflection on recent experiences / things you’ve learned

Personally, I like to intersperse my journal entries with lists, quotes, notes, ideas, poems, logs, etc. But if you prefer, you could keep a separate “Commonplace Book” for these things. The idea is just to write down anything that interests you. Don’t overthink it, they don’t have to be deep or even meaningful. I sometimes like to write things down that don’t entirely make sense to me in hopes that someday they will.

what to collect
  • Quotes from books, movies, essays, poems, conversations, videos, etc.
  • Song lyrics
  • Recipes
  • LISTS!!! God, I love lists.
  • Logs or trackers related to hobbies and interests

Unfortunately (for scrapbookers) a lot of physical ephemera has been replaced with digital alternatives. But lucky for us, we’ll always have trash.

Garbage can be a really interesting window into another time period. My grandma has a book crammed full of recipes cut out from 1950s magazines and packaging. I find it absolutely fascinating to see what people ate back then, and in many ways I feel like it helped understand her better. 

I like to hold onto garbage I find aesthetically pleasing or sentimentally significant, such as small flyers, tags, and wrappers.

from memory

If you like to draw, it can also be a great way to capture moments that otherwise go forgotten. I sometimes like to make one-page comics about my day. I’ll draw a panel about an overheard conversation, something I thought about while gazing at a view, the outfit I wore. These are some of the days I remember most clearly because I took the time to do so. For more examples of this, look up Hourly Comics Day!

from life

Portraits are a nice way to capture a friend’s essence. Drawings are sometimes a more accurate representation of a person’s presence than photographs.

The same is true for environments. Draw your bedroom, your favorite tree, your workplace, your favorite coffee shop. The act of drawing from life requires you to slow down and carefully observe your surroundings. This level of presence and focus is hard to come by in today’s fast-paced world.

It doesn’t have to be good. Even better if it isn’t. Sketchbooks come with a feeling of pressure to make every page perfect. I like to use an excessive amount of sticky notes and pasted-in paper to give myself a second chance at “warm-up” pages. Though if you can tolerate it, your future self will likely enjoy every drawing you offer them.

note

I like to keep a small pad of paper in my bag to draw and write in while I’m out in the world. It’s a nice way to keep my hands busy while waiting for the bus, and it keeps me off my phone. I sometimes tear out pages and tape them in my hodgepodge book or sketchbook if I really like them.

Collaborative works are a lovely activity and way to document relationships. I look back fondly on the pieces of paper my elementary school friends and I doodled all over when we were bored in class.

Zines are a fun way to do this (though you can also make them by yourself, of course.) A zine can be about anything you want, and you can scan them and make copies to share. They can contain writing, art, collage, etc. I sometimes like to take turns with a friend filling one page at a time, passing them back and forth until a story emerges.

correspondence

Letters and postcards are a wonderful way to stay in touch with long-distance friends. Phone calls and texts have their place, but there’s nothing like the joy of opening a letter. I stayed in touch with people this way during the pandemic, and I’m currently penpals with my kid cousins who live in Nova Scotia. I plan to hold onto these forever and ever. It’s sort of a unique form of communication because you just monologue to each other about your own lives, asking and answering questions here and there. Letters can contain other things, too. My cousin and I send each other heavily decorated envelopes full of stickers, comics, drawings, bookmarks, buttons, etc.

conclusion

I hope this zine inspires you to make something. Take these ideas, and make them your own! It’s your life, figure out whatever’s the most enjoyable and sustainable for you. I find a lot of fulfillment and comfort in documenting my life, but you might be different. If you’d rather let your brain choose what memories to hold on to and leave the rest to the ocean of time, so be it.
